5 Tips on Going to the Beach with Your Bike

If you only have a road bike, riding on the beach will be pretty much impossible. It’s easier to carry your bike than to conquer dunes. A rocky beach may be less complicated, but still difficult.

For regular beach rides, consider a Fat bike with 4-inch wide tyres. They prevent sinking and make pedaling easier. For occasional rides, a mountain bike with wide winter tyres and lower pressure improves grip and makes riding manageable.

Use lower gears when riding on soft sand to avoid the rear wheel getting stuck due to high torque. It may feel slow, but it helps maintain motion.

Keep a constant pace and avoid unnecessary braking or shifting. You’ll naturally stop when you stop pedaling. On dunes, lean back and lightly touch the brake lever to control speed.

Don’t shift your weight forward on sand—it slows you down and could cause a flip. Sit on the saddle and lean slightly back to give your rear tyre better grip.

Standing while pedaling on sand disrupts balance and sinks your wheels, making riding much harder.

Sharp turns in sand don’t work. Plan your moves early and use body lean instead of sharp handlebar turns. Turning abruptly will dig your wheel in and cause a crash.

Lock your bike where you can see it—like a volleyball pole (if unused) or a lifeguard station (with permission).

When swimming, ask someone nearby to watch your bike for a short while. Most people are happy to do so, especially if you return the favor.
